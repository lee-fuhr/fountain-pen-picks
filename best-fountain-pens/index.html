Quick answer

The LAMY Safari Fountain Pen is our top pick for 2026 because it offers a comfortable grip, reliable nib, and durable build at a very reasonable price.

The Pilot Metropolitan is an excellent runner-up with a classic look and smooth steel nib, while the Kaweco Sport is ideal for pocket carry and the Pilot Varsity is the best disposable budget option; the Waterman Hémisphère delivers a premium writing experience for those ready to invest more.

Fountain pens have seen a resurgence in recent years, and for good reason: they make everyday writing feel more deliberate and pleasurable. Whether you’re journaling, taking notes, or drafting letters, a fountain pen glides across the page with minimal pressure, reducing hand fatigue over long sessions. The key decisions come down to nib size (fine, medium, broad), grip comfort, filling mechanism (cartridge vs. converter vs. built-in reservoir), and overall build quality. Choosing the right fountain pen for 2026 means weighing your budget, portability needs, and willingness to maintain the pen. If you’re new to fountain pens, you’ll want something forgiving and easy to clean. If you’re a collector or daily writer, you might value heft, aesthetic, and precision. We’ve researched owner feedback and expert reviews to narrow the field to five standout models that cover every use case, from under ten dollars to a few hundred dollars.

The picks in detail

1. LAMY Safari Fountain Pen: The do-it-all default

Best overall
4.7out of 5

The LAMY Safari is the most versatile fountain pen you can buy for everyday use. Its triangular grip makes it comfortable for long writing sessions, and the snap-cap means you can jot a note in seconds. The steel nib is smooth, available in several sizes, and easily interchangeable.

Pros

  • Ergonomic, non-slip grip that encourages proper finger placement
  • Snap-on cap with a strong clip, ready in an instant
  • Interchangeable nibs let you swap sizes without buying a new pen
  • Durable ABS plastic body withstands daily carry and bumps

Cons

  • The triangular grip doesn’t suit everyone’s hand shape
  • Uses proprietary LAMY cartridges (or a converter, sold separately)
  • Limited nib width options compared to more premium models

Who it’s for

Anyone who wants a reliable, low-maintenance fountain pen for school, work, or daily journaling.

Who should skip it

You prefer a round, classic pen grip or want a smooth, wet writer right out of the box (the Safari’s nib can feel a bit dry on some papers).

2. Pilot Metropolitan Fountain Pen: The polished runner-up

Runner-up
4.6out of 5

The Pilot Metropolitan offers a refined metal-body design that feels more expensive than its price suggests. It writes reliably with Pilot’s smooth steel nib and comes in a range of classy finishes. The squeeze converter is included, so you can use bottled ink immediately.

Pros

  • Brass barrel gives it a satisfying heft and durability
  • Includes a squeeze converter for bottled ink, no extra purchase needed
  • Nib is very smooth for a steel nib, especially in medium
  • Classic, professional styling suits a business or office setting

Cons

  • The step-down from barrel to section can be uncomfortable for some grips
  • Only available in fine or medium nib widths (no broad or extra-fine)
  • Squeeze converter holds less ink than a standard piston converter

Who it’s for

Writers who want a fountain pen that looks and feels like a premium instrument without a premium price tag.

Who should skip it

You have a very high-handed grip or prefer a lightweight, plastic-bodied pen for travel.

3. Kaweco Sport Fountain Pen: The ultimate pocket companion

Best value
4.4out of 5

The Kaweco Sport is a compact, short pen that extends to full size when the cap is posted. Its octagonal shape prevents rolling, and the screw cap ensures it won’t leak in a bag. The nib is smooth and reliable, though it’s a small pen, so larger hands may find it a bit short when unposted.

Pros

  • Very pocket-friendly, just capped, posts to a full length
  • Screw-on cap seals tightly, preventing ink evaporation and leaks
  • Available in many colours and materials, from plastic to metal
  • Uses standard international cartridges and can take a mini converter

Cons

  • The unposted length is too short for most adult hands to write comfortably
  • The mini converter holds very little ink (same as a short cartridge)
  • Plastic version feels a bit light and cheap compared to metal-bodied rivals

Who it’s for

People who carry a pen in their jeans pocket or need a travel-friendly fountain pen that won’t dry out.

Who should skip it

You don’t like posting the cap to write, or you want a pen you can leave on your desk without capping every time.

4. Pilot Varsity Disposable Fountain Pen: Zero-fuss, all-function

Best budget
4.0out of 5

The Pilot Varsity is a sealed, disposable fountain pen that costs less than many rollerballs. It writes immediately out of the box with a wet, smooth fine nib, and you can refill it a couple of times if you’re careful. It’s the ideal introduction to fountain pens with no maintenance.

Pros

  • Truly disposable, no mess, no converter, no cleaning required
  • Extremely affordable, even cheaper than many disposable ballpoints
  • Writes immediately with a surprisingly smooth, wet flow
  • Can be refilled by pulling out the nib and feed (if you’re adventurous)

Cons

  • Not refillable in the traditional sense, once the ink runs out, most people toss it
  • Only available in fine nib (medium and broad are not offered)
  • The plastic body feels flimsy and the barrel is translucent, not elegant

Who it’s for

Beginners who want to try a fountain pen for the first time without any commitment or investment.

Who should skip it

You prefer a more substantial writing instrument or plan to use fountain pens long-term (buy a Safari instead).

Best for specific needs

Best for everyday writing

If you write for hours at a desk or in meetings, you need a pen that balances comfort, reliability, and nib options. The LAMY Safari’s triangular grip trained generations of students to hold a pen correctly, and its snap-cap makes it the quickest to deploy. With interchangeable nibs, you can switch from fine to broad without buying another pen. It’s the default choice for daily use across virtually any setting. Our pick: LAMY Safari.

Best for carrying in a pocket

When you want a fountain pen that disappears into a jeans coin pocket or a shirt sleeve, the Kaweco Sport is the clear winner. Its short, capped length and octagonal body prevent it from rolling off a desk or rattling in a bag. The screw cap seals tightly, so you can toss it in a backpack without worrying about leaks. Just remember to post the cap to reach a full writing length. Our pick: Kaweco Sport.

Best for first-time fountain pen users

Starting with a fountain pen can be intimidating, cleaning converters, buying ink, worrying about nib pressure. The Pilot Varsity eliminates all that. It’s disposable, costs less than a coffee, and writes smoothly from the first stroke. You can try different papers and writing styles without any investment. If you find you like the experience, you’ll be ready to graduate to the Safari or Metropolitan. Our pick: Pilot Varsity.

Our verdict

At the end of the day, the best fountain pen for 2026 depends on how and where you write. The LAMY Safari remains the most well-rounded choice for almost anyone, offering a comfortable grip, reliable nib, and durable build at a fair price. If you want a touch more elegance, the Pilot Metropolitan is a close second. The Kaweco Sport is unbeatable for portability, the Pilot Varsity is the best no-risk entry point, and the Waterman Hémisphère is the pick for those who appreciate craftsmanship and a classic silhouette. Whichever you choose, you’ll rediscover the simple joy of putting ink to paper.
