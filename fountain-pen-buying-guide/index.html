How to choose a fountain pen

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A great fountain pen for 2026 balances nib smoothness, filling convenience, and grip comfort within your budget. Focus on a fine or medium nib for everyday writing, and choose either a cartridge/converter pen for ease or a piston filler for larger ink capacity and lower waste.

What nib size should I start with?

For your first pen, a fine or medium nib is the safest and most versatile choice. Fine nibs put down less ink and work well on cheaper paper, while medium nibs offer a noticeably smoother, slightly wetter line that shows off ink shading. Extra-fine nibs can feel scratchy on less-smooth paper, and broad or stub nibs require high-quality paper to avoid feathering and bleed-through. Nib material matters too: most entry-level pens use steel nibs, which are perfectly reliable and often tuned well out of the box. Gold nibs add springiness and a slight bounce, but they cost significantly more, and you don’t need one for enjoyable writing. Focus on how a nib feels in practice: a well-adjusted steel nib can outperform a poorly adjusted gold nib every time.

What filling mechanism should I choose?

Three main options dominate the market: cartridge/converter, piston, and vacuum filler. Cartridge/converter pens are the most beginner-friendly, you can use pre-filled ink cartridges for immediate use without mess, or swap in a converter to use bottled ink. This compatibility makes them the easiest to clean and maintain. Piston fillers hold more ink and look elegant, but they require bottled ink and careful cleaning when switching colors. Vacuum fillers offer even higher ink capacity and a unique filling action, but they’re more complex to clean and typically cost more. For a first pen, a cartridge/converter model gives you the most flexibility with the least fuss.

How important is the grip section?

The grip section directly affects writing comfort over long sessions. Look for a section that is not too thin nor too thick for your hand size, most pens have a diameter roughly between 9 and 11 mm. Triangular grips help beginners maintain a correct hold, but many experienced writers prefer round, smooth sections that allow you to rotate the pen freely. Material matters too: metal sections can become slippery with sweaty hands, while textured plastic or resin sections offer more grip. Avoid grips with sharp step-downs near the nib, as those can cause discomfort after extended use. If possible, hold the pen before buying, or check owner reviews for mentions of grip comfort.

What materials and build quality should I look for?

Resin (plastic) is the most common material: it’s lightweight, warm in the hand, and available in endless colors and patterns. Lacquered brass or metal pens add heft and feel more durable, but they can become tiring over long writing sessions. Ebonite and urushi lacquer are premium materials prized for their texture and depth, found mostly in higher-priced pens. For daily carry, a resin pen with a screw-on cap is usually a good balance of weight and durability. Clip strength, cap seal (so the nib doesn’t dry out overnight), and overall fit and finish matter more than the price tag. A well-made pen from a reputable brand will last decades with basic care.

How do I clean and maintain a fountain pen?

Regular cleaning prevents clogs and keeps ink flowing smoothly. For cartridge/converter pens, simply run water through the nib and converter until the water runs clear. Piston and vacuum fillers require filling and expelling water several times. Avoid soaking the pen body if it has metal parts, as that can damage the finish. Flush your pen every time you change ink colors, or at least every few months if you stick with the same ink. Never use hot water or alcohol, which can warp the feed or dissolve plastics. A simple bulb syringe can speed up cleaning for many pens. If you leave a pen unused for more than a couple of weeks, empty and clean it first to prevent ink from drying and crystallizing in the feed.

What should a beginner budget for a first fountain pen?

You can get a very capable, reliable fountain pen for a budget-friendly price. At this level, you’ll find well-regarded steel nibs, decent resin bodies, and cartridge/converter compatibility. Stepping up to a few hundred dollars refines the materials, perhaps a gold nib, a piston filler, or a more decorative resin, but doesn’t automatically improve the writing experience. Rather than focusing on price alone, think about the features that matter most to you: nib smoothness, comfortable grip, and reliable ink flow. A pen that fits your hand and writes smoothly will serve you better than a more expensive model that doesn’t suit your style. Many enthusiasts find that their favorite writer is a middle-of-the-road model, not the most expensive one in their collection.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use any ink in a fountain pen?

Yes, as long as the ink is specifically labeled for fountain pens. India ink, calligraphy ink, and most drawing inks contain shellac or pigments that will clog the feed permanently. Stick to dye-based or pigment fountain-pen inks from reputable brands.

Do fountain pens leak in airplanes?

They can. Changes in air pressure can cause ink to be pushed out of the nib or converter. To prevent leaks, store the pen nib-up during flights, keep it nearly full (less air expands), or use a sealed pen case. Many travelers prefer cartridge pens because a sealed cartridge is less prone to leaks.

How long does a fountain pen last?

With normal care, a quality fountain pen can last decades. The nib and feed are designed to be long-lived; only dropping the pen nib-first or excessive force will cause permanent damage. Many heirloom pens from the 1940s still write beautifully today.

Is a fountain pen hard to write with?

No, you just need a light touch. Fountain pens require no pressure; the ink flows by capillary action. If you press hard as you might with a ballpoint, you may damage the nib or get a scratchy feel. A few minutes of relaxed writing is usually enough to adapt.

What paper works best for fountain pens?

Smooth, coated, or higher-weight papers prevent feathering and bleed-through. Look for paper labeled as fountain-pen-friendly, common brands include Rhodia, Clairefontaine, Tomoe River, and Leuchtturm. Avoid cheap printer or notebook paper, which can cause fuzzy lines and show-through.

Do I need a gold nib for good writing?

Not at all. Many excellent steel nibs write just as smoothly as gold nibs. Gold nibs offer a softer, springier feel and can be more forgiving of slight rotation, but they are not necessary for a pleasurable writing experience. Budget matters: if you’re starting out, a well-tuned steel nib will serve you beautifully.

In shortThe best fountain pen for you is the one you will actually use every day. Focus on nib size, grip comfort, and a filling system that matches your lifestyle. Don’t overthink the price or the brand, a modest pen that writes reliably and feels good in your hand is worth far more than an expensive pen that sits in a drawer.
